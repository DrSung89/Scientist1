A Computational Model of the Drosophila Brain: Unlocking Sensorimotor Processing

How modeling 50 million synaptic connections reveals the intricate logic of behavior.

1. The Era of Complete Connectomics

The assembly of the adult Drosophila melanogaster central brain connectome is a monumental milestone in neuroscience. Containing more than 125,000 neurons and 50 million synaptic connections, it provides an unprecedented template for examining sensory processing. Shiu et al. (Nature, 2024) created a leaky integrate-and-fire computational model of the entire Drosophila brain based on neural connectivity and neurotransmitter identity to study circuit properties of feeding and grooming behaviors.

2. Simulating Taste and Behavior

To examine the neural circuits that influence feeding, the researchers focused on Gustatory Receptor Neurons (GRNs) responding to sugar, water, bitter, and salt. The computational model accurately predicts that activation of sugar-sensing or water-sensing gustatory neurons activates neurons required for feeding initiation (proboscis extension). Interestingly, the model predicted that sugar and water pathways share common interneurons, functioning synergistically, while bitter stimuli strongly inhibit this extension.

3. In Vivo Validation

The power of a computational model lies in its ability to generate testable hypotheses. By using optogenetics (CsChrimson), the team validated these predictions in living flies. They found that out of 11 cell types computationally predicted to activate the motor neuron MN9 (responsible for rostrum extension), 10 actually elicited proboscis extension in vivo.

This study demonstrates that modeling brain circuits using only synapse-level connectivity and predicted neurotransmitter identity can accurately describe complete sensorimotor transformations.
