FROGS: How Deep Learning from NLP is Revolutionizing Drug Target Prediction

Applying the 'word2vec' concept to genes to extract functional meaning from OMICS data.

1. The Limitation of Gene Identity Matching

Large-scale OMICS investigations produce "gene signatures" represented as lists of gene candidates relevant to a biological activity.[1] Many machine learning applications currently rely on matching gene identities when analyzing these signatures. However, evaluating similarities purely by matching gene identities—conceptually similar to Fisher's exact test—ignores the underlying functional roles of the genes.[1] For example, early Natural Language Processing (NLP) analyses used one-hot encoding where words like "cat" and "kitty" were considered completely distinct.[1]

2. Introducing FROGS

To overcome this, Chen et al. developed an approach named Functional Representation of Gene Signatures (FROGS).[1] This technique acts like 'word2vec' for bioinformatics. FROGS vectors encode human genes' functions based on Gene Ontology (GO) and their empirical functions proxied by experimental expression profiles from ARCHS4.[1] In the embedding space, genes with similar biological functions are clustered together.

3. Enhancing Drug Target Prediction

The researchers applied FROGS to the Broad Institute's L1000 dataset to predict compound-target associations. By comparing the FROGS-based model to identity-based methods and other embedding schemes, they found that FROGS significantly outperformed existing methods in identifying shared pathways induced by co-targeting compound-shRNA/cDNA pairs.[1] Furthermore, by integrating additional pharmacological activity data sources (e.g., pQSAR, NCI60), FROGS predicted thousands of high-quality compound-target interactions that were previously missed.[1]
