Bypassing Paralysis: The Dawn of Brain-Spinal Interfaces in Primates

Exploring the limits of human technology through a review of Capogrosso et al.'s revolutionary Nature paper on restoring locomotion.

1. The Ultimate Technological Frontier

Spinal cord injury disrupts the communication between the brain and the spinal circuits that orchestrate movement.[1] For decades, repairing a severed spinal cord was considered science fiction. However, a 2016 study published in Nature by Capogrosso and colleagues showcased exactly how far human technology has advanced. By using a brain-spinal interface to electronically bypass the lesion, they successfully alleviated gait deficits after a spinal cord injury in nonhuman primates.[1]

2. Building the Brain-Spinal Bridge

The researchers established a wireless recording and stimulation platform.[1] Rhesus monkeys were implanted with an intracortical microelectrode array into the leg area of the motor cortex to record spiking activity.[1] They designed wireless communication modules that enabled control over the spatial and temporal parameters of epidural electrical stimulation (EES) with a latency of about 100ms.[1]

They utilized the entry points of the dorsal roots as the targeted anatomical landmarks that guided the design and positioning of spinal implants.[1] The researchers then developed a decoder that calculated the probability of foot strike and foot off events from cortical modulation to anticipate the activation of extensor and flexor hotspots.[1]

3. Restoring Locomotion Without Prior Training

After testing in intact monkeys, the team performed a unilateral corticospinal tract lesion at the thoracic level.[1] Remarkably, during the first week after lesion and without training of the monkeys, the brain-spinal interface restored weight-bearing locomotion on a treadmill and overground, improving both the quantity and quality of steps performed by the impaired leg.[1]

4. A Triumph of Engineering and Biology

This ecological approach enabled a smooth cooperation between residual supraspinal signals and the brain-spinal interface in generating leg movements.[1] The implantable components integrated in the brain-spinal interface have all been approved for investigational applications in similar human research, suggesting a practical translational pathway for proof-of-concept studies in people with spinal cord injury.[1]

This achievement is a profound testament to human ingenuity. By fusing neuroscience, electrical engineering, and advanced software algorithms, scientists have effectively hacked the nervous system to restore mobility.
