FDA Accelerated Approval: Endpoints, Confirmatory Trials, and Expedited Withdrawal

A deep dive into the updated FDA guidance on the accelerated approval pathway for serious conditions and the statutory changes introduced by FDORA.

1. The Evolution of the Accelerated Approval Pathway

The FDA's Accelerated Approval pathway was originally instituted in 1992 to expedite the availability of drugs that treat serious conditions and fill an unmet medical need. It allows approval based on a surrogate endpoint (e.g., tumor shrinkage) or an intermediate clinical endpoint that is reasonably likely to predict clinical benefit. However, the reliance on surrogate endpoints necessitates rigorous post-marketing confirmatory trials to verify the anticipated clinical benefit.

2. FDORA and Confirmatory Trial Mandates

Following the enactment of the Food and Drug Omnibus Reform Act (FDORA), the FDA has fundamentally strengthened its oversight. The updated guidance clearly specifies that the FDA may require a sponsor to have a confirmatory trial already underway prior to granting accelerated approval. This policy shift addresses historical criticisms regarding "dangling" accelerated approvals where confirmatory trials were delayed for years or never completed.

3. Expedited Withdrawal Procedures

Crucially, the revised statute establishes streamlined procedures for withdrawing an accelerated approval if the confirmatory trial fails to verify clinical benefit, or if the sponsor fails to conduct the trial with due diligence. The regulatory threshold for establishing clinical benefit in oncology, for example, heavily relies on demonstrating an improvement in Overall Survival (OS) or robust Progression-Free Survival (PFS).
