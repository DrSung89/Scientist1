Evaluating Contribution of Effect in Novel Cancer Drug Combinations

Reviewing the FDA draft guidance on clinical trial designs for combination therapies in oncology.

1. The Rationale for Combination Therapies

Combination therapy in oncology is an important treatment modality designed to target multiple therapeutic pathways to improve treatment response, minimize adverse events, or both. When pursuing a novel combination, a marketing application must demonstrate both the safety and effectiveness of the novel combination, as well as provide evidence to demonstrate that the individual components contribute to the treatment effect.

2. Factorial Clinical Trial Designs

To definitively prove the contribution of each drug, a factorial design randomized trial is highly recommended. Traditional evaluation of novel combinations is accomplished using a multi-arm, randomized trial that includes a combination arm, the monotherapy arms, and a standard-of-care arm.

When analyzing time-to-event endpoints from these multi-arm clinical trials, investigators can utilize tools like our Kaplan-Meier Survival Calculator to compute overall survival curves and Log-Rank P-values.

3. Using External Data Sources

In some cases, the conventional approach employing a standard factorial design may be infeasible. When appropriate, sponsors can use external data, such as prospectively collected patient-level data (e.g., registry data) or Real-World Data (RWD) sources, to supplement or replace single-agent arms.

When assessing whether an external data source is fit for purpose, sponsors should consider data provenance, extent of missing data, knowledge of the disease's natural history, and the availability of patient-level data. The guidance emphasizes that summary-level evidence from published studies should generally be considered only for hypothesis generating.
