Evaluating Contribution of Effect in Novel Cancer Drug Combinations

Insights into the FDA guidance on demonstrating the individual contribution of drugs in combination therapies to avoid unnecessary toxicity.

1. The Co-Development Challenge

The landscape of oncology treatment has rapidly shifted from monotherapies to rational drug combinations, particularly involving immunotherapies and targeted biologics. However, combining multiple investigational agents introduces complex regulatory and clinical challenges. The FDA's stringent "Contribution of Effect" requirement dictates that a sponsor must demonstrate that each drug in the combination provides a clinically meaningful contribution to the overall efficacy profile.

2. Trial Design Strategies: Add-on vs. Factorial Designs

To isolate the efficacy of individual components, the FDA guidance strongly recommends specific clinical trial designs. The most common is the "add-on" design (A+B vs. A alone), assuming drug A is the established standard of care. When co-developing two completely novel investigational drugs, a more rigorous factorial design (A+B vs. A vs. B) is often required to rule out the possibility that one of the drugs is merely adding toxicity without therapeutic benefit.

3. Pharmacological Rationale and Biomarkers

Empirical evidence of synergy or additivity must be established early. The guidance emphasizes that strong mechanistic rationale—often supported by validated biomarkers—is required before initiating late-stage trials. Understanding drug-drug interactions (DDI) and overlapping toxicity profiles is essential for determining the optimal dose and schedule of the combined regimen.
