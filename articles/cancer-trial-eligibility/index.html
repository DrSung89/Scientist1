Broadening Eligibility in Cancer Clinical Trials: Washout, Meds, and Performance Status

An analysis of the FDA's draft guidances on modernizing oncology trial eligibility criteria to improve diversity and real-world applicability.

1. The Problem with Restrictive Criteria

For decades, oncology clinical trials have relied on highly restrictive eligibility criteria to isolate the effect of an investigational drug and protect patient safety. However, this practice systematically excludes a vast portion of the real-world cancer population—such as the elderly, those with mild organ dysfunction, or patients taking concomitant medications for common comorbidities. The resulting clinical trial cohorts are often homogenous, limiting the generalizability of the efficacy and safety data.

2. Key Areas of Modernization

To address this critical flaw, the FDA has released specific draft guidances urging sponsors to broaden trial eligibility. Key focus areas include:

  • Washout Periods: Reducing the arbitrary waiting periods between prior therapies and trial enrollment, relying instead on pharmacokinetic properties to determine safe intervals.
  • Performance Status: Encouraging the inclusion of patients with an ECOG performance status of 2, rather than restricting trials strictly to patients with an ECOG of 0 or 1.
  • Organ Dysfunction & Concomitant Medications: Allowing patients with controlled comorbidities or mild renal/hepatic impairment to enroll, provided there is no mechanistic rationale for elevated risk.

3. Impact on Real-World Evidence (RWE)

By liberalizing these criteria, clinical trials will accrue patients faster and reflect the true heterogeneity of the patient population. This shift ensures that the safety and efficacy data generated during Phase II and III trials are far more representative of the clinical reality, ultimately improving real-world clinical decision-making and patient outcomes.
