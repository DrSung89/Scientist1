Broadening Eligibility in Cancer Clinical Trials: Washout, Meds, and Performance Status

Examining the FDA's draft guidances on modernizing oncology eligibility criteria to improve trial diversity and generalizability.

1. The Need to Modernize Eligibility Criteria

The primary purposes of eligibility criteria for cancer clinical trials are to select the intended patient population and reduce potential risks to participants. However, unnecessarily restrictive criteria may slow patient accrual, limit access to trials, and lead to clinical results that do not fully represent treatment effects in the patient population that will ultimately use the drug. Appropriately broadening cancer trial eligibility criteria can improve the generalizability of trial results. For statistical validation of these broader populations, researchers frequently rely on our Kaplan-Meier Survival Calculator.

2. Rethinking Washout Periods and Concomitant Medications

A washout period is a treatment-free period between the most recent anti-cancer treatment and the initiation of the investigational drug. Time-based washout periods should be scientifically justified. The FDA guidance recommends that relevant clinical and laboratory parameters should be used in place of time-based washout periods to address safety considerations. Furthermore, patients using concomitant medication should only be excluded from trial participation when clinically relevant known or predicted drug-drug interactions (DDIs) and potential overlapping toxicities will impact the safety of trial participants.

3. Re-evaluating Performance Status (PS)

Many trials exclude lower-functioning patients based on the Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) and Karnofsky (KPS) scales.

The FDA advises that patients with ECOG PS2 (or KPS 60-70) should be included unless there is a scientific and/or clinical rationale for exclusion justified by established safety considerations.
